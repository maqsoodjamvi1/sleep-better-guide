If your laptop has become a permanent fixture on your duvet, I am willing to bet your sleep schedule is currently running on chaos. I learned this the hard way during my second year of full-time remote work. Somewhere between answering Slack messages in my pajamas and closing my last tab at 11:30 PM with the glow of the screen illuminating my pillows, my internal clock completely shattered.

Psychologists call this circadian drift. I call it the creeping blur where your bed stops being a sanctuary for rest and turns into just another satellite office. When you work from where you sleep, your brain loses its ability to associate the mattress with winding down. Fixing this does not require overhauling your entire life or buying expensive sleep gadgets. It requires drawing hard physical and mental lines in a tiny apartment or a house where the temptation to stay horizontal is always within arm’s reach.

The Psychology of the Laptop Pillow Fort

When you sit up in bed with your laptop, you are training your brain to stay alert in the exact place it needs to switch off. Evolutionarily speaking, your brain is a pattern-matching machine. It relies on environmental cues to know what hormones to release. Desk equals focus. Kitchen equals fuel. Bed equals melatonin and deep muscle relaxation.

When you blur those lines, you send mixed signals. You might finish a stressful spreadsheet while leaning against your headboard, and twenty minutes later, wonder why your heart is racing when you try to close your eyes. Your nervous system is still waiting for the next shoe to drop because the workspace and the sleep space are the exact same four-foot radius.

Step One: Establish a Fake Commute

When you don’t leave your bedroom to start your workday, you miss out on the physical transition that signals the start of the day. Without a commute, your brain never gets the memo that it is time to shift gears.

You need to manufacture a commute, even if it is just a walk around the block before you open your email. Put on real shoes—not slippers—and walk outside for ten minutes. This serves a double biological purpose: morning natural light hits your retina, which stops melatonin production and anchors your circadian rhythm for the next sixteen hours. When you walk back inside, bypass your bedroom entirely and set up your workspace at a kitchen table, a counter, or a dedicated desk.

Step Two: Build a Hard Closing Ritual

Drift usually happens at the end of the day. You finish working at 5:00 PM, but you leave your laptop open on the nightstand. By 8:00 PM, you are “just checking one last thing,” which turns into a two-hour scroll or project review.

To stop this, you need a physical shutdown routine that mimics locking up an office building.

  • Shut down the machine: Literally power down your laptop and put it inside a drawer, a closet, or a bag out of sight.
  • Clear the perimeter: Remove all chargers, notebooks, and pens from the sleeping area.
  • Change your clothes: Swap your daytime lounge pants for actual nightwear, signaling to your skin and senses that the workday is dead.
  • Dim the overheads: Switch off harsh overhead lights an hour before bed and turn on a warm, low-level lamp across the room.

Reclaim the Mattress

If your bed has become your office, you might need to evict yourself from it during waking hours entirely. For the first week of breaking this habit, make a rule: no sitting on the bed between 8:00 AM and 10:00 PM, unless you are changing the sheets. If you want to read a book during the day, sit in a chair. If you want to take a mid-afternoon break, lie on the couch.

It feels restrictive at first, but retraining your brain is a lot like training a pet. You have to be stubbornly consistent with the boundaries. Within a few days, you will notice your body actually remembering how to get heavy and tired the moment your head hits the pillow at night.

As you start untangling your work life from your sleep sanctuary, think about what small physical change you can make today to put distance between your laptop and your sheets. Taking that first step toward resetting your environment makes all the difference in how you rest tonight.
